How sensory organs communicate with the nervous system

Sensory organs detect aspects of the world—light, sound, chemicals, pressure, temperature—and convert them into signals the nervous system can process. This process begins with specialized receptor cells (or specialized nerve endings), which respond to specific forms of input.[1]

When activated, these receptors generate electrical signals that travel through sensory neurons into the nervous system, where the signals are relayed, filtered, and integrated across multiple levels of processing in the brain.[1]

Why this process is not identical in any two people

The number, density, genetics, and responsiveness of sensory receptor cells vary significantly across individuals.[2][3][4][5] These differences affect how signals are detected at the very first stage.

The neural pathways and cortical organization that process these signals vary as well, shaped by development, learning, experience, and ongoing neural plasticity.[6][7]

As a result, the pathway from sensation to perception is not identical in any two people.[2][3][6]

What this means for experience

Although humans share broadly similar sensory systems, each individual’s system is configured differently in its biological structure and neural processing.[2][6]

This means that sensory perception is shared in general form, yet necessarily distinct in its finer details for every person.[2][3][5][6][7]
